## Why this time is different

The AI Act isn't just another European regulation. It's the <span className="corail">world's first comprehensive legal framework</span> for artificial intelligence, classifying AI systems by risk level: unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal.

Good news for SMBs: most of your use cases (chatbots, writing assistants, analytics tools) fall under "limited" or "minimal" risk. The obligations are proportionate.

The catch: even at limited risk, you have **transparency obligations**. If a customer interacts with an AI chatbot, they must know. If you use generative AI to produce content, same rule.

## The 4 risk levels, translated for an SMB

**Unacceptable risk**: banned across Europe since February 2025. Social scoring, behavioral manipulation, real-time facial recognition in public spaces. You're probably not concerned.

**High risk**: strictly regulated. AI that filters CVs, decides on credit, grades a student, controls access. Many SMBs use these tools <span className="corail">without realizing it</span>, through HR software or commercial scoring tools. If that's your case, technical documentation, risk assessment and human oversight become mandatory.

**Limited risk**: transparency obligation. Chatbots, text or image generators, deepfakes. The user must know they're interacting with an AI or that the content has been generated. This is where 80% of SMB use cases land.

**Minimal risk**: no specific obligation. Spam filters, spell checkers, productivity tools with no impact on individuals.

The trap isn't at the extremes. It's at the border between limited and high risk: the moment your tool touches hiring, employee scoring, or service access, you move up a category.

## The real timeline, no jargon

Three dates structure your action plan.

**February 2, 2025**: prohibitions are already active. Unacceptable-risk practices have been banned since that date. That's behind us.

**August 2, 2025**: obligations for general-purpose AI models (GPAI). This concerns providers like OpenAI, Anthropic or Mistral, not SMB users directly. But your AI tool providers are impacted, so their terms of service are shifting.

**August 2, 2026**: full entry into application for high-risk systems and transparency obligations. That's the date that concerns you directly. National supervisory authorities will be operational from then on.

## Sanctions, to make it sting

The fines provided by the AI Act are not symbolic.

Up to **35 million euros or 7% of global annual turnover** for prohibited practices. Up to **15 million euros or 3%** for violations of high-risk obligations. Up to **7.5 million euros or 1.5%** for providing incorrect information to authorities.

For an SMB, the conversation will be about percentages rather than millions. But 1.5% of your annual revenue is still <span className="corail">a very bad day</span>.

## What Europe has planned for SMBs

The AI Act doesn't forget small businesses. Three support mechanisms are built in.

**Regulatory sandboxes**: each country must create at least one by August 2026. <span className="corail">SMEs get priority access, free of charge</span>. It's a space to test your AI usage and verify compliance, guided by competent authorities. In France, the CNIL and Arcom are working on the national rollout.

**Simplified documentation**: the European Commission will publish SME-friendly technical documentation templates. No law firm required. These templates will be made freely available.

**Proportionate fees**: compliance and registration costs must account for company size. Sanctions too, to a degree.

## The 5 actions to launch before August 2026

1. **Map your AI uses**. List every tool used in the company, even the free ones, even those used occasionally by a single employee. Include AI features built into your existing software (Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, Notion, etc.).
2. **Classify each use** by risk level using the 4 categories. A three-column table is enough: tool, use, risk level.
3. **Write an internal AI policy**. Usage rules, allowed data types, decisions that must remain human, procedure when in doubt. Two pages maximum so it gets read.
4. **Train your teams**. Not a 2-hour lecture on the history of AI. A practical workshop on your concrete cases: how to use Copilot without leaking customer data, how to verify the output of a text generator, when to say no to the tool.
5. **Document decisions**. Trace who approved which use, when, on what criteria. In case of inspection, your documented due diligence is what protects you.
