## Why a policy, not a ban

The temptation is to ban AI outright. "No AI at the office." Simple, clear, and <span className="corail">counterproductive</span>.

Your best people are already using AI. They save time, they're more effective.

Banning it means losing that advantage and pushing them to do it secretly, with free tools, personal accounts, and <span className="corail">zero guardrails</span>.

A policy does the opposite: it says **yes, but with rules**. It protects the company while enabling initiative.

## The 5 rules that matter

After working with several SMBs on this, we've distilled the essentials into <span className="corail">5 rules</span>.

Not 5 questions. Not 5 vague principles. **5 first-person statements** that any employee can read, understand and sign in **2 minutes**.


### Rule 1: I only use <span className="corail">approved tools</span>

The company maintains a list of approved tools: for example ChatGPT Team, Claude Pro, Copilot 365. In these tools, conversations are **not used to train the AI**.

Outside this list, it's forbidden by default.

Why it matters: free versions of ChatGPT or Claude don't offer the same confidentiality guarantees as enterprise accounts. A free account is an <span className="corail">open pipe</span>.


### Rule 2: Outside approved tools, I never paste <span className="corail">identifying data</span>

No client name, no bank details, no social security number, no signed contract, no financial statement, no password.

When in doubt, replace with "Client A", "Amount X" before pasting.

This is the simplest and most effective rule. It protects against **90% of risks** without blocking usage.

### Rule 3: I <span className="corail">verify</span> before I send

AI makes things up. That's its nature.

Before sending a response to a client, signing a document, or publishing content, I check **the numbers, proper nouns, and legal references**. If the AI cites a source, I open it.

A fabricated revenue figure in a business proposal, a non-existent legal article in a formal letter. These mistakes happen. They're avoidable with **30 seconds of verification**.


### Rule 4: <span className="corail">I decide</span>, AI suggests

An HR decision, a binding commercial offer, a refusal, a diagnosis: these remain a **human act**.

AI helps prepare, it doesn't sign in your place.

This is also a regulatory requirement. The **EU AI Act** requires human oversight for any AI system involved in significant decisions.


### Rule 5: When in doubt or after a mistake, I <span className="corail">speak up</span>

If an employee has a doubt about usage, if they've pasted sensitive information by mistake, if they receive a suspicious message (cloned voice, phishing email, wire transfer request): they contact the **AI officer**.

<span className="corail">No penalty for reporting.</span>

This clause makes all the difference. Without it, incidents get hidden. With it, they surface and everyone learns.

## 3 mistakes to avoid

**The 20-page policy.** Nobody reads it. The operational memo fits on <span className="corail">one page</span>. The detailed policy (GDPR, AI Act, incident procedure) is a separate reference document for the leader and AI officer.

**Listing tools by name without principles.** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini... the list changes monthly. Set **principles** ("no client data in unapproved AI tools") and maintain the tool list separately.

**Never reviewing it.** AI moves fast. Plan a <span className="corail">revision trigger</span>: new major model release, internal incident, regulatory change. At minimum, annual review.
