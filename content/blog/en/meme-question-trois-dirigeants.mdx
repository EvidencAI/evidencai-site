## The demo that shifted the room

Tuesday April 7, 9:30 AM, Valence. Seven SMB leaders around the table. Day 1 of the *Deciding with AI* training. On the morning's agenda: understanding how an AI generates text, and how a leader should talk to it to get something useful back.

I open a small lab I'd prepared the weekend before. A web page, an input field, a button. I ask the room: *"Give me a real question you're asking yourself right now."* One of the leaders throws: *"How do I retain my customers in 2026?"*

I type. I hit the button. Three answers appear simultaneously on the screen, side by side. Same question. Three different roles given to the AI behind the scenes: *"You are an HR consultant"*, *"You are a creative director"*, *"You are a financial analyst"*.

The faces change.

## Three universes, not three shades

The **HR consultant** talks about the symmetry of attention: customer-facing teams that are well trained, well treated, and stable produce a customer experience that builds loyalty on its own. They mention service rituals, turnover that breaks the relationship, the culture of hospitality. Vocabulary: team, posture, consistency. Tone: human, structuring.
The **creative director** talks about memorable moments, brand storytelling, personalizing touchpoints, turning every customer contact into a micro-experience worth sharing. They mention ambassador programs, surprise, the small detail that lands. Vocabulary: story, emotion, signature. Tone: mobilizing, almost theatrical.

The **financial analyst** talks about CAC/LTV ratio, churn rate, RFM segmentation, measurable loyalty programs. They mention margin on recurring customers, the hidden cost of a lost customer, the ROI of a commercial gesture. Vocabulary: cohort, retention, trade-off. Tone: dry, numerical.

These are not three versions of the same answer. They are **three universes**. Three reading grids. Three points of view that, side by side, look like the content of an executive committee.

In the room, one sentence came back several times during the break: *"I'll never ask a question the same way again."*

## The naked prompt trap

Here's what struck them: until then, they'd open ChatGPT or Claude, type their question, hit enter, read the answer. That's it. They had **never** told the AI who it was supposed to be.

Without a role, the AI answers with the average of everything it knows. It produces the answer *everyone* would write. Not the one *you* need. You walk away vaguely disappointed, without knowing why.

That's what I call the naked prompt. A question without a frame. And that's what the vast majority of leaders using AI every day do.
## The technique in 2 lines

Before your question, add one sentence:

> *"You are a [specific role], answer from this angle."*

That's it. Two extra lines, and the answer changes completely.

## Why it works

Technically, AI predicts each word based on the preceding context. The role you give it doesn't change the chosen words *a little*. It reconditions **the entire probability distribution** at every word. And this effect compounds at every following word.

Over a 400-word answer, you get three texts with nothing in common. Not because the AI is creative. Because the starting frame was radically different.

Translated for a leader: the role weighs far more than you'd expect in the answer. Often more than the text of the question itself.

## Multi-angle: 3 roles, 1 executive committee

The real value isn't in *one* answer with a role. It's in **three simultaneous answers, with three different roles**, to the same question.
You ask the question to an HR person, a salesperson, a finance person. You read all three. You arbitrate.

You're not delegating the decision to the AI. You're instrumenting your own internal debate. You see angles you'd have missed alone. You identify tensions between departments without having to convene them.

In three minutes, you get what an executive committee produces in an hour. Not to replace the committee. To **prepare it**.

## Try it now, no setup needed

The good news: **you don't need anything beyond what you already use**. No tool to install, no account to create. Claude, ChatGPT, Mistral, whichever.

1) Write your usual question.
2) Ask it three times in three separate conversations, starting each time with *"You are a [role], answer from this angle."*
3) Re-read all three. Arbitrate.

Try it on a decision you need to make this week. You'll see.

## Going further: the API key

If you want to ask the same question to several roles in parallel and display answers side by side without copy-pasting three times, there's a small technical step: the **API key**.
It's a string of characters provided by the AI vendor (Anthropic, OpenAI, Mistral), which lets a small tool (a web page, a spreadsheet, a script) query the AI automatically. You create one in three clicks on the vendor's website. It costs a few cents per use.

With an API key, three roles run in parallel, in 10 seconds, side by side. That's what unlocks the whole multi-angle approach at scale. And that's exactly what I showed on Tuesday.

## Takeaways

Without a role, you get the average. Useful once, disappointing ten times. With a role, you get a point of view. Stronger, but it's a single angle. With three roles, you get a debate. That's where decisions are built.

Next time you open Claude or ChatGPT, don't type your question directly. Start with one line: *"You are a..."*. You'll see what the seven leaders saw on Tuesday.
